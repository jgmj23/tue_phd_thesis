\chapter{Background and Unifying Concepts}
\label{ch:background}

The paper-centric chapters that follow span photonic devices, quantum-key
post-processing, post-quantum cryptographic acceleration, datacenter transport,
and security observability. Read individually, they appear to belong to separate
research communities, each with its own models, metrics, and implicit assumptions.
This chapter provides the shared vocabulary and the analytical lens that make them
a single dissertation. It advances a cross-layer view of secure communication in
which a design choice or operational constraint at one layer is judged by its
consequences at the others.

The chapter first introduces that cross-layer lens, describing how information
value, deployment constraints, and adversary models necessitate a multi-tiered
response to the quantum threat. It then presents the data processing unit (DPU) as
the common hardware substrate that connects cryptographic primitives to the
infrastructure. Following this framework, it establishes the detailed background
for each of the three parts of the thesis---quantum key distribution (Part~I),
post-quantum cryptography (Part~II), and programmable datacenter infrastructure
(Part~III)---in the order in which they appear in the body. Finally, it synthesizes
these concepts to show how they constrain and enable one another.

\section{The Cross-Layer Security Stack}
\label{sec:bg-stack}

The introduction framed the quantum threat not merely as an algorithm-selection
problem, but as a systemic architectural challenge driven by ``Harvest Now,
Decrypt Later'' (HNDL) adversaries. Because the cost of storing and eventually
decrypting intercepted traffic is immense, the threat model must account for the
decay rate of information value. Unconditionally high-value, slowly decaying secrets---such
as state infrastructure keys or long-lived intellectual property---justify
the deployment complexity and bespoke hardware of physics-based security, primarily
quantum key distribution (QKD). The photonic devices that enable QKD are difficult
to scale, but they offer information-theoretic security that is immune to algorithmic
advances.

In contrast, the much larger volume of computationally protected traffic---ranging
from session tokens to standard web traffic---decays rapidly. It is served by
post-quantum cryptography (PQC), which replaces vulnerable public-key mechanisms
with new mathematical hard problems believed to resist both classical and quantum
algorithms. PQC must operate at datacenter scale, requiring entirely different
optimization strategies than QKD. Yet, both physics-based and computationally-based
protections must ultimately run on the same programmable network fabric.

To analyze this heterogeneous landscape, this dissertation structures the response
along three distinct but interacting layers, illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:cross_layer_stack}.

\begin{itemize}
  \item a \textbf{device layer}, where photonic sources and the physical channel
        determine the quality and stability of the quantum states that security
        protocols can assume. The governing quantities here are physical: spectral
        purity, free spectral range, quality factors, and loss budgets.
  \item a \textbf{protocol layer}, where QKD post-processing and post-quantum
        algorithms turn raw physical or mathematical resources into usable key
        material and authenticated channels. The governing quantities here are
        algorithmic and informational: key distillation rates, security margins,
        computational complexity, and message sizes.
  \item an \textbf{infrastructure layer}, where programmable network hardware,
        datacenter transport, and confidential-computing environments decide
        whether those protocols can actually be deployed, accelerated, observed,
        and defended at scale. The governing quantities here are operational:
        latency, throughput, determinism, queue depth, and observability.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Cross-Layer Method}

The recurring method of the thesis is to take a security or performance
assumption stated at one layer and test what happens to it when it is mapped
onto another. An isolationist view often hides systemic vulnerabilities. For
instance, a protocol proof might assume perfect single-photon sources, while the
device layer actually exposes multiphoton states subject to thermal drift. A
post-quantum signature scheme might be highly efficient when benchmarked on a
single idle core, but its tail latency might collapse under heavy datacenter
batching. A transport layer might be provably encrypted by MACsec or IPsec, yet
still leak critical application state through traffic ordering and timing behavior.
Finally, an infrastructure defender operating a confidential-computing cluster must
detect intrusions without seeing the payload, violating the assumptions of classical
intrusion detection systems.

Research questions RQ1--RQ3 each target a single layer---PQC deployment, QKD
architecture, and photonic-source design. RQ4 binds them by exposing these
cross-layer failure modes. The remainder of this chapter supplies the necessary
background for each layer to make these interactions legible.

\section{Programmable Network Infrastructure for Security Offload}
\label{sec:bg-dpu}

The single piece of hardware common to nearly every contribution in this thesis is
the data processing unit (DPU). It is introduced first because it underpins the QKD,
PQC, and infrastructure chapters alike.

SmartNICs and DPUs represent a paradigm shift in datacenter architecture. They extend
the network interface from a passive I/O endpoint---responsible only for moving packets
between the wire and host memory---into an active, programmable execution environment.
In modern DPU architectures like the NVIDIA BlueField series, the device features a
cluster of general-purpose Arm CPU cores, dedicated hardware accelerators (including
inline cryptographic engines for AES-GCM and public-key acceleration), substantial
on-board memory, and programmable packet-processing pipelines \textsc{TODO: citation (BlueField/DOCA architecture)}.
In more advanced configurations, DPUs provide direct GPU-connected datapaths (e.g., GPUDirect RDMA)
that bypass the host CPU entirely.

This architecture makes it possible to place security-critical operations---such as
key distillation, cryptographic encapsulation, storage encryption, telemetry collection,
and firewall policy enforcement---at the edge of the host rather than deep inside its
operating system~\cite{meyer2026hqc}. By moving these functions to the DPU, the host CPU
is freed to run tenant workloads, and the infrastructure provider retains a secure,
isolated domain to enforce policies even if the host is compromised.

\subsection{The Offload Decision Framework}

However, offload is not automatically beneficial. Moving a task from a powerful,
high-frequency x86 host CPU to a lower-power Arm core on a DPU introduces significant
overheads: data movement across the PCIe bus, queueing delays, synchronization locks,
resource contention among DPU cores, and additional software complexity. The host CPU
will almost always win a raw single-thread benchmark.

Therefore, the useful question is never whether a DPU is ``faster'' in isolation. The
relevant architectural questions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Partitioning:} Which specific part of a pipeline should move to the DPU?
    \item \textbf{Batching:} Under what traffic conditions and batch sizes does the offload break even?
    \item \textbf{Bottlenecks:} What becomes the new system bottleneck once the compute task has moved?
    \item \textbf{Determinism:} How stable is the latency distribution under heavy, concurrent load?
\end{enumerate}

These questions recur in identical form across different domains in this thesis. They
dictate how QKD post-processing scales, whether post-quantum key encapsulation meets
handshake latency budgets, and how secured edge-AI pipelines manage image I/O. For this
reason, the DPU is treated here as a unifying substrate rather than as a detail of any
single chapter.

Furthermore, the same programmability that enables offload also transforms the DPU into
a natural observation point. Placed inline between the network and the host, the DPU
can inspect traffic metadata, connection timing, and queue depths without host interference,
a capability that becomes the foundation of the confidential AI observability discussed
in Part~III.

\section{Quantum Key Distribution and Integrated Photonic Sources}
\label{sec:bg-qkd}

Part~I treats quantum-secured communication from the device layer upward, addressing
both the physical generation of quantum states and the classical protocol logic required
to use them.

\subsection{Integrated Photonic Sources}

Integrated photonics addresses the device-level bottleneck in deployable quantum
communication. Traditional tabletop optics, with discrete non-linear crystals and
mirrors, are too fragile, bulky, and expensive for wide-scale deployment. Photonic
integrated circuits (PICs) aim to miniaturize these components onto a semiconductor
chip using fabrication processes compatible with existing CMOS or telecom infrastructure.

For entanglement-based QKD, the photon source is the critical component. It determines
the spectral structure, brightness, purity, and long-term stability of the photon pairs
that every subsequent protocol stage consumes~\cite{Wehner2018}. If a source emits
multiphoton pulses or indistinguishable spectral modes drift due to temperature fluctuations,
the visibility of quantum interference drops, the quantum bit error rate (QBER) rises,
and the protocol may fail to generate secure keys.

The photonic-source work in this dissertation focuses on microring resonators as
candidate sources for frequency-bin entanglement via spontaneous four-wave mixing (SFWM).
In a microring, light circulates and interferes constructively only at specific resonant
frequencies separated by the Free Spectral Range (FSR). Key physics and engineering
considerations include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Quality Factor ($Q$):} A measure of the resonator's photon lifetime.
    High-$Q$ rings generate highly coherent, narrow-linewidth photons but are extremely
    sensitive to fabrication variations and thermal drift.
    \item \textbf{Thermal Tuning and Stability:} Refractive indices are temperature-dependent.
    Maintaining precise alignment with standardized telecom grids requires continuous,
    active thermal tuning, which can inadvertently affect the FSR and disrupt entanglement symmetry.
    \item \textbf{Spectral Purity (Schmidt Number):} A mathematically pure bipartite entangled
    state has a Schmidt number of 1. Real sources suffer from spectral correlations
    that degrade purity, necessitating precise dispersion engineering or tight filtering.
    \item \textbf{Platform Tradeoffs:} Silicon nitride ($\text{Si}_3\text{N}_4$) offers ultra-low
    loss and broad transparency but lacks the strong electro-optic (Pockels) effect of
    lithium niobate ($\text{LiNbO}_3$), meaning the choice of material dictates the tuning mechanisms
    available~\cite{Kumar2015,Mao2018,Grebenchukov2023}.
\end{itemize}

These device-derived quantities are precisely the parameters that determine the budget
available for coexistence with classical traffic. They define the point at which the
device layer becomes legible to the network layer above it.

\subsection{QKD Post-Processing}

A deployable QKD system is far more than a quantum channel. The quantum channel only
exchanges raw, error-prone measurement outcomes. A classical post-processing pipeline
is required to distill these raw detections into secure, usable symmetric keys~\cite{Xu2020}.
This pipeline consists of several computation-heavy stages:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Sifting:} Alice and Bob discard measurements where they used incompatible bases.
    \item \textbf{Error Estimation:} A subset of bits is publicly compared to estimate the QBER,
    bounding the maximum information an eavesdropper could have gained.
    \item \textbf{Information Reconciliation:} Forward error correction, typically using large
    Low-Density Parity-Check (LDPC) codes or Cascade, resolves discrepancies between Alice's and Bob's strings.
    \item \textbf{Privacy Amplification:} A universal hash function compresses the reconciled string
    to erase any partial information the eavesdropper holds, yielding the final secure key.
\end{enumerate}

All classical communication during these steps must be strictly authenticated to prevent
man-in-the-middle attacks.

Traditionally, these stages run on general-purpose host CPUs. However, as datacenter
links scale to 400~Gbps and beyond, dedicating host cycles to key distillation becomes
prohibitive. Furthermore, the latency introduced by software-based LDPC decoding undermines
the ultra-low-latency transports, such as RDMA, that modern datacenters rely on.

The scalability challenge of QKD is therefore an integration challenge: how can a
comparatively low-rate, computation-heavy post-processing pipeline be offloaded onto
programmable network hardware? The goal is to produce quantum-secured key material
efficiently enough to seamlessly feed line-rate datacenter encryption without starving
the host CPU. This requirement connects the integrated photonic source directly to the
DPU substrate discussed in Section~\ref{sec:bg-dpu}.

\section{Post-Quantum Cryptography and Deployment Constraints}
\label{sec:bg-pqc}

Post-quantum cryptography (PQC) represents the computationally secure counterpart to QKD.
It replaces the integer factorization and discrete logarithm problems vulnerable to Shor's
algorithm with entirely different mathematical structures believed to resist both classical
and quantum cryptanalysis.

The transition focuses on two main primitive types: Key Encapsulation Mechanisms (KEMs),
which establish a shared symmetric key over an insecure channel, and Digital Signatures,
which provide authentication and non-repudiation.

\subsection{Algorithmic Families and Characteristics}

The standardized and candidate algorithms studied in Part~II vary drastically in their
underlying mathematics, leading to profound differences in computational structure,
memory footprint, and message sizes \textsc{TODO: citation (NIST FIPS 203/204; HQC selection)}.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Lattice-Based Cryptography:} Algorithms like ML-KEM (formerly Kyber) and
    ML-DSA (formerly Dilithium) rely on the hardness of the Module Learning With Errors (MLWE)
    problem. They offer an excellent balance: fast execution times, moderate key and ciphertext
    sizes, and efficient hardware implementations. However, their reliance on polynomial sampling
    and rejection sampling introduces variable timing that must be carefully managed to avoid
    side-channel leaks.
    \item \textbf{Code-Based Cryptography:} Algorithms like HQC (Hamming Quasi-Cyclic) rely on
    the hardness of decoding random linear codes. While their encryption and decryption operations
    are fast, they suffer from significantly larger public keys and ciphertexts compared to lattices.
    Additionally, their operations involve large, dense bit-vector multiplications that have distinct
    memory access patterns~\cite{meyer2026hqc}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Deployment and Architectural Constraints}

For networked systems, the migration to PQC is not finished when a NIST standard is published.
A mechanism that executes efficiently in a single-threaded benchmark may behave disastrously
under datacenter provisioning constraints.

Integrating PQC into protocols like TLS 1.3 or IPsec requires transmitting much larger
public keys and ciphertexts during handshakes. This can trigger IP fragmentation, increasing
latency and packet-loss probability. Furthermore, datacenters handle thousands of connections
per second. KEM decapsulation and signature verification must therefore be deeply batched.
Lattice schemes, with their variable-time rejection sampling, batch poorly on SIMD architectures
because a single long-running sample stalls the entire vector lane.

Consequently, the central questions are architectural: where should the cryptographic work run,
how should requests be batched, how much latency can the service mesh tolerate, and which
memory buses become bottlenecks? These are the questions that motivate offloading PQC onto
the DPU substrate.

These questions are tested most sharply not on isolated primitives, but inside complete
application pipelines. For instance, the edge-AI setting examined in Part~II involves a
pipeline where post-quantum protection, image I/O from sensors, and GPU-based inference
all compete for the same datapath. In such scenarios, the theoretical efficiency of a PQC
algorithm matters less than how gracefully its offload degrades when it must coexist
with real application workloads.

\section{Programmable Datacenter Fabrics and Confidential AI}
\label{sec:bg-infra}

Part~III turns to the infrastructure layer itself. It is here that the physical assumptions
made by QKD protocols, and the mathematical assumptions made by PQC algorithms, are either
upheld or quietly violated by the reality of datacenter networking.

\subsection{RDMA over High-Throughput Optical Fabrics}

Modern datacenters---especially those built for distributed AI training and high-performance
computing---interconnect GPUs, DPUs, and storage using Remote Direct Memory Access (RDMA),
commonly deployed as RDMA over Converged Ethernet (RoCEv2) over high-throughput optical fabrics.

RDMA achieves its massive throughput and microsecond latency by bypassing the host operating
system networking stack entirely. Applications interact directly with the NIC hardware via
Queue Pairs (QPs). However, RDMA's performance and security properties rest on specific
assumptions about message ordering and completion semantics. While strict ordering is guaranteed
\emph{within} a single Queue Pair, ordering \emph{across} different Queue Pairs is much weaker
and heavily dependent on the fabric's load-balancing and optical-switch behavior \textsc{TODO: citation (RoCEv2/RDMA ordering specification)}.

When these lower-layer ordering and timing behaviors diverge from what the higher-layer
application assumed, vulnerabilities emerge. For example, a channel that is provably
encrypted via IPsec or MACsec can still expose exploitable structural information if an
attacker observes the timing and out-of-order arrival of RDMA completions. This is a classic
instance of the cross-layer failure mode at the heart of RQ4, and forms the core of the
RDMA security analysis in Chapter~\ref{ch:rdma}.

\subsection{Confidential Computing and Observability}

As AI models become astronomically expensive to train, protecting the model weights and
training data from unauthorized access becomes critical. Confidential computing addresses this
by using hardware-based Trusted Execution Environments (TEEs), such as AMD SEV-SNP or Intel TDX,
combined with strictly encrypted memory and PCIe links \textsc{TODO: citation (confidential computing/TEE overview)}.
This ensures that the tenant's workload is protected even from a privileged hypervisor or a
malicious infrastructure operator.

However, the same cryptographic boundary that shields tenant data also blinds the defender.
In a confidential AI training cluster, payloads are entirely opaque to the infrastructure provider.
Classical Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS), which rely on deep packet inspection and payload
signature matching, are rendered useless.

The background question for the final part of this thesis is therefore: what observable signal
survives the encryption and TEE boundaries? The answer lies in metadata, traffic structure,
packet timing, and hardware telemetry extracted directly from the DPU. By combining these
side-channel signals, it is possible to detect threats, such as data exfiltration or malware
beaconing, without ever accessing the payload. This reframes the DPU of Section~\ref{sec:bg-dpu}
from merely an accelerator into a privileged vantage point, extending the thesis focus from
prevention and acceleration toward detection and response.

\section{From Background to Contributions}
\label{sec:bg-synthesis}

The three layers introduced in this chapter are deliberately not independent. Their dependencies
form strict, unidirectional chains that dictate system design:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Device to Protocol:} Source-level spectral purity and FSR stability dictate
    the baseline error rates. These error rates determine the size of the LDPC matrices required
    in QKD post-processing.
    \item \textbf{Protocol to Infrastructure:} The matrix dimensions and the determinism of
    PQC rejection sampling dictate compute latency. This latency determines whether the algorithm
    can be placed inline on a DPU without starving the line-rate datapath.
    \item \textbf{Infrastructure to Protocol:} The ordering, timing, and visibility properties
    of the RDMA fabric determine whether the mathematical protections of the cryptography actually
    survive deployment against a network-level adversary.
\end{itemize}

This cross-layer perspective is the reason the dissertation combines topics---photonics, cryptography,
and datacenter networking---that would otherwise appear separate. The unifying question that runs
through every following chapter is how secure communication systems can be made scalable and
deployable when the binding constraints are distributed across physical, mathematical, and
operational boundaries. The body of the thesis now develops this layer by layer, beginning
at the foundation with the integrated photonic source.
